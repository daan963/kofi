Where is the Calgary Stampede Park?

Whether on foot or by car, the Calgary Stampede Park is centrally located southeast of downtown Calgary along MacLeod Trail S.E. between 12 Avenue southeast and 25 Avenue southeast.  Visitors can utilize public transportation and parking options all year long.

PRCA Rodeo Performances

Two main characteristics determine a rodeo. That’s the competitors’ skill and not forgetting the quality of the bulls, horses, and steers. There are world-class rodeo competitors and animals displayed at Stampede Rodeo. At the World’s Largest Outdoor Rodeo, cowboys and cowgirls face off every afternoon at 1:30 p.m. in a display of skill and grit that builds towards Showdown Sunday.

Stampede Rodeo Events

The PRCA (Professional Rodeo Cowboys Association) is the governing body of the Calgary Stampede Rodeo, which includes both judged and time events performances. Competition between cowboys and cowgirls is usually for the highest score or shortest time. It’s a fun rodeo competition that attracts several cash prizes to be won by the top athletes, who also get commemorative belt buckles. 

Here are the six Pro Rodeo performances at the Calgary Stampede 2022.

  • Bull Riding

Bull Riding at Calgary Stampede is a world-renowned spectacle in which cowboys pit their nerves against a bull. The ropes wrapped around the bull are thickly braided, complete with a handhold. When the ride is over, cowbells are hung from the rope to allow it to fall when it has been weighed down. Bull riders hold the rope tight by gripping it tightly. To avoid breaking loose before the end of the ride, he should avoid straightening his arm and keeping his hand from breaking loose. Cowboys don’t need to move their feet, and staying on is hard enough. However, if they spur, they get higher marks.

  • Saddle Bronc

Saddle Bronc is about maintaining the rhythm between a cowboy and his horse.

Riders move their feet in time with a bronc’s bucking action to the back of the saddle coming from the horse’s neck. Using a braided rein attached to the horse’s halter, the cowboy balances himself by holding onto it. It’s possible to pull him over the front, especially when his hand placement is too low. If the hand placement is too high, he may be thrown off the back before the eight seconds are up.

  • Barrel Racing

The rider and the horse must be in close partnership and possess control and attention to detail when Barrel Racing. A contestant races across the scoreline in a cloverleaf pattern by circling three barrels. The winner is determined by a short time, such as a hundredth of a second measured with an electronic eye timer.  Although riders may touch a barrel, they will receive a five-second penalty if they knock one over.

  • Steer Wrestling

During this event, cowboys compete against the clock and must demonstrate strength and coordination. Steer wrestlers wait behind a rope barrier on horseback. They are given a 12-ft head start when the steer is released upon showing a nod. He chases the steer with his horse, then drops to the ground and grabs its horns, fighting to control it. He needs to roll the steer on the ground, and it’s must be on its feet. The time is recorded as soon as the animal is on its side with all legs extended.  The steer must be kept running straight with the help of an additional horse, ridden by a hazer.

  • Bareback

Riders hold on to leather handholds as they extend their feet forward, holding onto riggings, which are pads wrapped around the bucking horse’s girth. In a spurring motion, the rider pulls his feet back toward the rigging. A higher spur means a higher score. Cowboys’ arms are heavily stressed as they absorb most of the horse’s power, and they are required to hold on for at least 8 seconds.

  • Tie-Down Roping

Tie-Down Roping is where a cowboy with his partner must rope and tie an animal.  It’s one of the oldest ranching tasks that involve speed, skill, and finesse. On horseback, the cowboy waits behind a rope barrier. He then gets 10 feet of a head start when a nod is given to release the calf. To rope the calf, the cowboy must dismount from his horse, reach it on the run, turn it on its side, and tie three of the animal’s legs. A cowboy’s hands are thrown in the air when the time is called, and the calf must be released immediately.
